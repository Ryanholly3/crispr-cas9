Learn CRISPR-cas9

So you want to learn CRISPR-cas9, huh? Slow down there. Maybe we should learn the basics of cell biology and DNA first. Scroll down to learn the background on these topics, and then take a look at the CRISPR-cas9 section. You'll be an expert geneticist in no time!

Wait, what is a cell?

A cell is the most basic unit of life! All living things are made up of cells - you, your dog, your favorite succulent, and even bacteria in your gut... ew. All animal cells have a basic layout, and share some basic components. One of the most important component of a cell, is the cell's nucleus. Inside the nucleus, the cell protects DNA - the instruction manual that tells the cell how to make all of its components!

DNA stores information inside of genes. Make sure you understand the difference between DNA and genes! DNA is composed of bases (sort of like letters) and forms a large string. A gene is a region of DNA that codes for a cellular component. About 1000 genes reside on a single DNA strand. Humans have about 25,000 genes! Genes vary between people. Almost All of the variation we see in humans, comes from differences at the level of genes!

Now that you understand what a gene is, let's move on to DNA editing.

DNA editing

Ever since Gregor Mendel discovered genes in 1866, biologists have DREAMED of being able to manipulate genes. But in order to get there biologists needed to better understand DNA. Biologists eventually figured out how gene editing could theoretically be performed. First, DNA must be cut at the site to be edited. Next, template DNA must be provided that contains the edits. The reason why this works, is that having broken DNA is a life-or-death situation for a cell. The cell has a repair mechanism in place to fix broken DNA - it searches for DNA that resembles the DNA at the breaksite. If successful, it "grabs" that DNA, and copies off of it to bridge the DNA break. If the template is different from the original strand. An edit could be made! Despite understanding how this would work - Biologists struggled to accomplish the first step, cutting DNA at a specific place!

Now that you know the basics to of gene editing, move onto CRISPR-cas9! You're almost an expert!!

Crispr-cas9

CRISPR-cas9 finally solved the problem and gave biologists a means to cut DNA at a specific place! While studying a bacterial defense system against invading viruses, Jennifer Doudna at UC Berkeley discovered CRISPR-cas9. She found that bacteria have a sort of immune system against viruses. When viruses attack bacteria, viruses inject their DNA into the bacterial cell. If the bacterial cell survive the attack, they save a copy of a piece of the viral DNA, and add into into their own DNA, in a CRISPR (an abbreviation for Clustered Regularly Interspaced Short Palindromic Repeats). If the bacterial cell comes in contact with that viral DNA again, the bacteria uses it to target the viral DNA for destruction! A bacterial protein, Cas9, binds to a copy of the CRISPR DNA, finds the match, and cuts it at a very precise site. This prevents the virus from being able to propagate. Biologists quickly realized that they could use cas9 in the lab to target any piece of DNA to be cut, as long as they could make a guide for cas9.
